Research

Transport machinery of the bacterial cell envelope

The bacterial envelope is a fortress of lipids and proteins. We work out — at near-atomic resolution — how the machines that build and cross it actually work, and how that knowledge might be turned against the pathogens that depend on them.

Mycobacteria include some of the world's deadliest pathogens, with Mycobacterium tuberculosis responsible for over a million deaths every year. Despite their importance, we still understand very little about how mycobacteria move substances across their unusual, multi-layered cell membrane.

Our aim is to discover and characterise the proteins that reside in the mycobacterial outer membrane. By combining structural biology, microbiology and biochemistry, we uncover the molecular mechanisms behind the transport of molecules in and out of the cell — and, in the longer term, hope this opens new avenues for antibiotic discovery.

Mycobacterial secretion systems

The mycomembrane is one of biology's most formidable barriers. We study the type VII secretion systems that export proteins and lipids across it — the same machinery that lets M. tuberculosis establish infection.

RND transporters & the MmpL family

MmpL proteins are lipid-handling pumps of the RND superfamily. Our cryo-EM structures of an MmpL homodimer are beginning to reveal how these transporters recognise their cargo and move it across the membrane.

Pigment transport in E. coli O157

Aryl polyene pigments shield the gut pathogen EHEC O157 from oxidative attack. We're mapping how an MmpL-family transporter and Lol-like periplasmic chaperones deliver these protective pigments to the cell surface.
